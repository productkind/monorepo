# Voice corpus analysis: how Kinga writes

Source: close read of all 20 published Substack articles in `thoughts/assets/articles/` (2026-07-09). Articles 001–014 are from her weekly-publishing era (2025); 015–020 are the most recent and weigh most as her current voice. Use this as positive evidence of the voice: drafts should sound like these quotes, not merely avoid the banned list.

## Sentence rhythm

Medium, comma-chained sentences that unspool the way speech does, often with a trailing qualifier. Comfortable comma splices: "I want to buy a new laptop, I'm based in the EU" (008). Short sentences mark emotional beats, not manufactured drama: "I felt a rush of sadness. I liked the guy." (001); "It's an inspiring place." (020). Occasional fragments when the story earns them: "While women didn't." (020); "No clarity. No alignment. And no one responsible for facilitating collaboration." (011).

## Openings

First-person, in-scene or plain timeline, often landing in dialogue within three lines:
- "It was a warm February day, I was sitting in a tiny meeting room" (001)
- "A couple of years ago, I was sitting in a meeting room in Berlin." (009)
- "The other day I was scrolling through LinkedIn when I saw yet another bold claim: 'No need for PMs, because PRDs are dead.' I stared at the screen, irritated." (011)
- "I started building with Lovable at the beginning of February this year." (015)
- Series pieces open with a direct welcome and a concrete promise: "Welcome to your Friendly Guide to AI Prompting! ... It's bite-sized and practical." (006)

## Closings

A modest wish plus a genuine invitation, usually a question:
- "I'm curious, have you ever struggled with defining an MVP of your product or new feature?" (016)
- "Try rewriting an email using the 'Define Tone and Style' technique and let me know how it goes!" (006)
- "I'd be intrigued to hear what techniques you use... Let's learn from each other." (008)
- Warm sign-offs: "Happy building!" (017), "Until then, happy practicing!" (007), "Thanks for being here, Kinga" (020)

## Connective tissue

"So," is the dominant hinge, narrative and logical: "So, when I got back to Lisbon, I had a session with her." (009). Also "Now,", "Then,", "At the time,", "Of course,", "Even though," as sentence openers, and "Well," for concessions: "Well, that isn't the case for us." (016). Sections pivot on questions to the reader: "What happened?" (020); "So how do you do all this?" (012). Narrative time markers: "By the summer... In the autumn..." (020).

## Signature constructions

- Honesty markers: "To be honest," (015), "But to be perfectly honest," (020), "It's weird to admit, but" (020), "Call me idealistic, but..." (020)
- Self-Q&A: "Did I enjoy coaching people? Yes; the nice ones." (020); "So, was I still a product manager, even though I wasn't actively managing a product?" (019)
- Reassurance idiom: "It's totally okay to handle it once several users mention it" (004); "And that's totally okay." (003); "I've been there." (003)
- Self-labelling: "I'm the type of person who loves to prepare for things" (009); "I'm impatient" (013)
- "please do" requests: "please do give feedback" (015), "please do leave a comment" (020)
- Hedged intensifiers: "quite a bit of reflecting" (020), "works pretty well" (015), "really" and "actually" as default hedges ("it was actually harder than I thought", 016)
- Scripted dialogue as a teaching device: "Annie, why did you score it 3?" (002)
- Imperative mottos in advice sections: "Prioritise ruthlessly." (004)

## Humour and asides

Dry, embedded, frequent; self-deprecating without performing:
- "These are my drawings, please don't laugh…" (017)
- "I said out loud, to no one but my sofa" (011)
- "(not insignificantly) pays the bills" (020)
- "Teresa Torres ended up being my mentor, of course, without her knowing it." (018)
- "I know, I hate to toot my own horn, but it's essential." (001)
- "that feeling you get from a mosquito bite – you desperately want to scratch it, but you know it's a bad idea" (013)

## Reader address

"I" for story, "you/your team" for guidance, "we" for shared human tendencies, often welded: "We want to be nice, we want others to like us – I certainly do." (013). Direct imperatives in advice ("Tell it to build an umbrella in detail", 015). Occasional endearments: "dear Reader" (001), "So, friends" (006), frequent "folks". Genuine questions to the reader mid-article and at the close.

## Punctuation

- **Spaced en dashes ( – ) are her aside marker**, used in nearly every article: "product team – developers, designers, product managers – regarding the outcome" (002). No em dashes.
- Semicolons appear naturally: "Yes; the nice ones." (020)
- Scare quotes for borrowed words: a "spaceship", "just", "revenue streams"
- Warm exclamation marks: "Let's dive in!" (004, 008), "Exactly!" (009)
- Question-form headings: "Why did I start a Substack?" (001), "So, How Can You Create a Nourishing Culture?" (012)
- Bold micro-headings and bolded imperative-first paragraphs in how-to pieces; ❌/✅ as do/don't icons
- **Zero emoji in all 20 articles.** Warmth comes from asides and exclamation marks.

## English

Consistently British (colour, prioritised, realised, sceptical, instalment, practising, meagre). Small non-native quirks stay in and are part of the texture: "Users wanted to see how the course looked like" (016), "elevates moral and motivation" (012), comma splices throughout. Formal connectors appear occasionally ("Therefore,", "Having said this,", 008).

## Rulings on conflicts with the banned list (Kinga, 2026-07-09)

Her corpus uses several banned-list items. She ruled:
- **"actually": allowed.** Her natural hedge.
- **Mid-piece rhetorical questions: allowed.** Core transition device. (Still banned as opening hooks.)
- **"Not X, but Y": stays banned**, even though the corpus uses it. Deliberate correction of a habit she's leaving behind.
- **"matters": stays banned**, same reason.
