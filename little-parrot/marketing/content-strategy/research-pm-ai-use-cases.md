# Research: AI in PM workflows (input for the PM productivity course)

Researched 2026-07-04. Distilled from a focused research pass over primary
sources (Lenny/Figma survey n=1,750, State of Product Management 2026,
Productboard CPO survey, Teresa Torres, Aakash Gupta, Peter Yang, Sachin
Rekhi, Marily Nika) cross-checked against our own user insights. Course
premise: PMs are told to use AI but don't know where it fits their workflow.

## The premise, evidenced

- 51% of product leaders say AI is leadership's top priority, yet only 2% of
  orgs prioritise talent development for it; Productboard calls the result
  "pilot purgatory" (85% buy tools, training skipped).
- Usage is near-universal (96% of PMs use AI frequently) but shallow: only ~6%
  of orgs treat it as core capability; 37% are stuck at "limited workflows".
  The open question in the market is not "should I", it's "where does it fit".
- 57% of workers hide their AI use from managers or colleagues; 42.6% of PMs
  name lack of training as a barrier. Women get less employer AI training
  (49% vs 79%) and less encouragement (61% vs 83%) - the course premise and
  the mission intersect here.
- Our own data asked for exactly this: "how PMs can use AI beyond content
  creation... for processing data... and generating insights faster"
  (Michelle); technical-conversation confidence (Corina).

## What works (ranked by evidence strength)

1. **Customer feedback and interview synthesis** - biggest demand gap in the
   Lenny survey (4.7% use it as primary today, ~32% want to). 47.1% of PMs
   summarise feedback with AI. Works when the PM owns the raw material and
   verifies; Torres's discipline: human pass first, AI as an extra
   perspective, check every quote against source.
2. **PRD / brief drafting from own notes** - the #1 current PM use (21.5%);
   reported 4h to under 2h. Verifiable because the PM knows the feature.
3. **Meeting summaries into stakeholder updates** - 30 to 10 minutes,
   same-day proof; review needed (notetakers miss informal commitments).
4. **Prototyping for discovery** - 19.8% current vs 44.4% desired; "idea to
   clickable prototype in under an hour". Caveat: demo value isn't user value.
5. **Competitive/market research** - 3h to ~30min; must check sources.
6. **AI as critic of your own draft** - "surprisingly good at critiquing a
   strategy once written" (Rekhi); highly verifiable; teaches judgement.
7. **Distilling long threads/debates before responding** (Yang's workflow).
8. **Data analysis** - weakest evidence for non-technical PMs; hard to verify
   a wrong number.

Time savings are real: 63% of PMs report saving 4+ hours/week; 55% say AI
exceeded expectations. And 92.4% report at least one significant downside.
The honest story: worth it, but you must verify.

## What fails (the guardrails to teach)

- Outsourced interview synthesis: ~30% of AI-cited "direct quotes" were wrong
  or fabricated (Torres); summaries can miss 20-40% of important detail.
- One-shot strategy drafting: "competent but generic B-level strategies"
  (Rekhi); documented burns from unedited output (outdated data, missed
  pivots).
- Skill atrophy: teams that outsource synthesis lose the ability to judge AI
  quality and act less on research.
- The inversion: PMs use AI most for output (PRDs, comms) but want it most for
  input (research, synthesis), which is exactly where hallucination is worst.
  Teaching *safe* synthesis sits on that gap.

## The consensus criteria for a good first activity

(a) the PM owns the raw material, so errors are catchable; (b) output is
verifiable in minutes; (c) the task is frequent and disliked, so time saving
shows within a week; (d) AI drafts or critiques, the human decides.

**Recommended first two activities:** (1) distil something you already own -
paste a messy feedback thread or your own meeting notes and turn them into
themes or a stakeholder update; (2) AI as critic - hand it a doc you wrote
and ask it to find the holes. Both are same-day wins with full verifiability.
Then PRD drafting from own notes, then feedback synthesis with the
verification discipline, then prototyping.

## Course positioning notes

- Differentiator: nobody teaches the verification and judgement layer; it is
  also our stated belief (AI doesn't do the thinking). "Fit AI into your
  week without losing your judgement" beats another prompt list.
- Marketing hooks with evidence behind them: "told to use AI, no guidance"
  (51% vs 2%), "using AI in silence" (57%), 63% save 4+ hours/week.
- Continues from "Write Better with AI" (prompting foundations for PMs);
  demand is explicitly "beyond content creation".
