The five questions come from Tayla Burrell's belief-interview prompt. They exist

## 1. What do you want to be known for in 3 years?

**Draft answer:** The person who made building with AI feel possible and calm
for women who don't come from a technical background. When a woman with a
business idea wonders whether she could build it herself, someone in her
network says "start with Little Parrot". Known for teaching the practical how,
step by step, in a kind register, when most of the industry only shows off
results. And for productkind being proof that a two-person company can run a
real product business with AI doing the production work.

*Evidence: the Little Parrot mission and ethos; the April Dunford post ("a
future where AI and technology let people automate the work they don't enjoy");
the June calendar throughline ("how a two-person company runs like a bigger
team with AI").*

## 2. What do you believe about your topic that most people in your space would disagree with?

**Draft answer:**
- Learning AI can feel calm and unhurried. Most of the industry sells urgency
  and fear of falling behind; we think fear is a bad teacher and possibility is
  a better motivator.
- AI should not write your content or do your thinking. The expertise is yours;
  AI helps with format and speed. Plenty of the "automate your content" crowd
  disagree.
- The goal of learning AI is not more output. Output is not value; judgement is
  the job that remains when building gets cheap.
- You don't need to become a developer to build software. The workflow of one,
  yes; the identity, no.

*Evidence: the April Dunford post, "Output is not value" (post 2), "When
building is cheap, judgment is the job" (post 3), the frame-AI-not-software-dev
rule.*

## 3. What makes you genuinely angry about how your industry operates?

**Draft answer:**
- Results shown without method. Conference stages and LinkedIn feeds are full of
  "AI made us 10x faster" with no practical steps anyone can take home. A result
  you watch is not a method you can repeat, and it leaves people feeling behind
  when the missing piece is the method, not their ability.
- "Use AI" mandates with no guidance, which turn AI into another anxiety task on
  someone's list.
- History repeating itself: in the early 1980s women were entering computer
  science faster than men until personal computers were marketed to boys. AI is
  being marketed the same way now, and only about one in five vibe coders are
  women.
- Manufactured urgency as a sales tactic aimed at exactly the people who are
  already overwhelmed.

*Evidence: the mtpcon anchor post (hype-vs-practical gap), the productkind
ethos, the Little Parrot mission.*

## 4. What have you figured out the hard way that your audience hasn't yet?

**Draft answer:**
- Start small and iterate. Perfect is the enemy of good, and not planning before
  prompting means many more iterations. (Our learners rediscover this in every
  course.)
- Specificity drives quality: context, audience, tone, length. The prompting
  recipe took years of product work to internalise and transfers in minutes.
- A surprising result is information, not a judgement on you. Years of shipped
  features nobody used and usability tests that went sideways taught this.
- What the work is worth beats how the work is done when you talk to people who
  hold budgets (and buyers of any kind).
- Great days at work are the ones spent making something; it's possible to
  build a working life around that, even at two people.

*Evidence: posts 4, 5 and 6 from the mtpcon series; the user-insights synthesis
showing learners internalising "start small" and the prompting recipe.*

## 5. What do you never want to be associated with?

**Draft answer:**
- Hype and guru positioning: "game-changing", curiosity-gap hooks, urgency
  countdowns, "everyone who doesn't learn AI will be replaced".
- Overselling outcomes: promising what a course doesn't teach.
- Sounding superior to other builders or to the tools people use; we frame the
  learner's pain, never bash the tool.
- Fear-based selling of any kind, especially aimed at women who are already
  told they're behind.
- AI-generated sameness: content nobody could trace back to a person.

*Evidence: the banned-language list in the tone-of-voice skill, the
dont-oversell and comics-dont-bash-tool rules, the April Dunford post.*
