# Post 6: The 15-minute user test

**Pillar:** the method, shown, with the confidence angle.
**Research trace:** showing work to a real person is the fear moment, and a
script makes it survivable (confidence-by-design); "that's the app's fault,
not yours" models the judgement-free register we're known for;
observation-before-interpretation is real PM craft shared plainly; testing
before polishing protects credits and weeks. Repackages the User Test
Script and Question Bank toolkit item.

## Format

**Spec key for the designer:** In the slide table, any text introduced as
"Prompt:" (or "prompt Lovable:", "Say:", "Assembled:") or written with
markdown symbols (##, -, 1., backticks) is a prompt snippet: literal text
the learner types into a tool. Render it verbatim in monospace inside a
prompt-input mockup (a chat input field with a cursor and send arrow), so
it reads as typed text rather than decoration; markdown symbols are part of
the typed text, never styled labels. Everything else in "Text on slide" is
display copy in the brand style.

**Carousel, 7 slides, 1080 x 1350 (4:5 portrait).** Script style: a
timeline down the side (2 min opening, 5 min task, 5 min questions, 2 min
close), dialogue in speech bubbles, observation notes on paper-scrap cards.

| Slide | Visual | Text on slide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 (cover) | A kitchen-table scene: one phone, two cups of tea, a notepad | "Your first user test needs one honest friend, 15 minutes, and this script. 👇" |
| 2 | Speech bubble, warm | "The opening line does the heavy lifting: 'There are no wrong answers. If something is confusing, that's the app's fault, not yours. The honest truth is what helps me improve it.' Now they have permission to be critical." |
| 3 | A task card, a zipped mouth emoji | "Give a goal, never a tour: 'Imagine you run a book club and want to suggest a book for next month. Try to add a suggestion.' Then stay quiet. If they get stuck, don't point; ask 'What are you looking for?' The answer is your fix list." |
| 4 | Two note cards side by side | "Write what you SEE, not what you conclude. 'She tapped the header three times' is an observation. 'She couldn't find the button' is an interpretation. Capture the observation first; it holds more information." |
| 5 | Three question bubbles | "Then three questions: 'What were you trying to do, and how did it go?' · 'What frustrated or confused you?' · 'What would make this more useful for you?' If they say 'nothing', ask: 'Was there a moment you paused?'" |
| 6 | An observation note with an arrow to a prompt card | "Afterwards, observations become prompts: 'She scrolled up and down three times looking for Add Book' becomes 'Move the Add Book button to the top of the page, above the list. Make it large and clearly labelled.'" |
| 7 (CTA) | Brand card, a thank-you note | "The full script and question bank are in Build a Valuable Product: How to Break Down Your Idea for Vibe Coding, on LittleParrot.app. First challenge free 🟪 Save this 🔖" |

**Alt text (for the post):** A user-test script carousel: an opening line
that gives permission to be critical, a task with no explanations, writing
observations rather than interpretations, three follow-up questions, and
turning observations into Lovable prompts.

## Caption (exact)

Watching someone use your app for the first time is uncomfortable, and it's some of the fastest learning you can do on your product. A script keeps it kind for both of you. 💛

It starts with one sentence that sets the tone: "If something is confusing, that's the app's fault, not yours." Then you give a goal instead of a tour, and you watch: where do their hands hesitate, where does their face crease? Write down what you see rather than what you conclude. "She tapped the header three times" will still be true tomorrow; "she couldn't find the button" is already a guess.

Fifteen minutes, three questions at the end, and you leave with a to-do list your users wrote for you.
