# Post 4: Activities, steps, details, and the MVP line

**Pillar:** the method, shown (PM expertise, shared).
**Research trace:** "break a feature into testable steps" is our clearest
comprehension miss across courses, and story mapping is the framework that
teaches it; the map is what makes one-feature-at-a-time prompting possible
(the credit-saving habit); teaches the real PM term (user story mapping)
with a plain structure. Repackages the User Story Mapping Cheat Sheet
toolkit item.

## Format

**Carousel, 7 slides, 1080 x 1350 (4:5 portrait).** Sticky-note style: a
wall of sticky notes building up layer by layer, colour-coded per layer
(activities, steps, details), the Book Club Organiser as the example.

| Slide | Visual | Text on slide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 (cover) | A sticky-note wall, three colour bands | "Between 'I have an idea' and 'build it' there's a map. Product teams call it user story mapping, and it takes one pack of sticky notes. 👇" |
| 2 | Top row of sticky notes highlighted | "Layer 1, ACTIVITIES: the big things your user wants to accomplish, across the top. 'Set up book club', 'Choose next book'. Name the goal, so 'Choose next book' beats 'Book voting system'." |
| 3 | Second band of notes under one activity | "Layer 2, STEPS: how she does each activity, left to right, as short verb phrases. Under 'Choose next book': Suggest books → Browse suggestions → Vote → See the winner." |
| 4 | Third band, small notes under one step | "Layer 3, DETAILS: the small, buildable pieces at each step. Under 'Suggest books': Enter title and author. Add a cover image. Add a recommendation note. Each detail starts with an action word: these become your prompts." |
| 5 | One note being rewritten | "One rule keeps the whole map honest: every note describes what the USER does, never what the system does. 'Send confirmation email' becomes 'Receive confirmation email'. It keeps you planning her experience instead of your machinery." |
| 6 | A horizontal line drawn across the wall | "Then the most satisfying move in product planning: draw one horizontal line across the map. Above the line: your first version. Below: everything that can wait. You've scoped an MVP." |

**Alt text (for the post):** A user story mapping carousel: activities
across the top, steps beneath each activity, buildable details at the
bottom, every note written from the user's perspective, and a horizontal
line separating the first version from later work.

## Caption (exact)

The gap between "I have an idea" and "what do I prompt first?" is where most app projects stall. User story mapping is the product-team tool that closes it, and it works just as well on a kitchen table. 🗺️

Three layers of sticky notes. Activities across the top: the big things your user wants to accomplish. Steps beneath: how she does each one, left to right. Details at the bottom: the small buildable pieces, each starting with an action word. Those bottom notes are your future prompts, one focused feature at a time, which is exactly how you avoid the vague mega-prompt that burns credits.

And when the map is done, you draw one horizontal line across it. Above the line is your first version. Below it, everything that can wait. Scoping decisions rarely feel that clear.
