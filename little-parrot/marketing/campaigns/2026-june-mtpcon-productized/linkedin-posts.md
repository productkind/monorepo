# LinkedIn posts — mtpcon London & Productized Lisbon (June 2026)

Single-insight posts, woven by theme across both conferences. Grounded voice (no hype, no curiosity-gap hooks, no em dashes), drawn from session notes and Kinga's own observations.

Suggested order: lead with Post 1 as the anchor, then space the rest over 2–3 weeks.

---

## Post 1 — The hype-vs-practical gap (anchor post)

I spent last week at two product conferences, mtpcon in London and Productized in Lisbon. Almost every talk had a moment where the speaker showed how AI had made them faster: more pull requests, more products shipped, weeks of work done in an afternoon.

Not one of them showed how. There were no practical steps I could take home and try on Monday.

Then I'd turn to the person sitting next to me and hear the opposite story. Their company had told them to "use AI", but given no guidance on what for. So using AI had become another task on the list. Use it for something, anything, just to be seen using it.

That gap is the whole problem. A productivity win you can't reproduce is a highlight reel. And telling a team to "use AI" with no worked example is pressure dressed up as direction.

If you lead a team and you want them using AI, show them one real thing you did with it this week. The prompt, the steps, the bit that went wrong. One concrete example does more than any mandate.

## Post 2 — Output is not value

I keep thinking about one number from this week's conferences. John Moriarty from Intercom shared that his team had tripled the pull requests each person produces, and shipped nine major launches in two months. The room was impressed. So was I, at first.

Then I caught myself. Pull requests and product launches are things we make. None of those numbers tell me whether anyone is using the result, whether it made a customer's life easier, or whether it brought in any revenue.

Rich Mironov put it more bluntly in his talk: code only becomes product when someone pairs it with positioning, pricing and a reason to care. Shipping faster just gets you to that test sooner.

It's an easy trap, because output is satisfying to count and value is slow to measure. I've fallen into it myself, mistaking a busy roadmap for a useful one.

So before celebrating how much faster your team ships, it's worth asking the harder question: of everything we shipped last quarter, what did people use, and what did we build that no one touched?

## Post 3 — When building is cheap, judgment is the job

A theme ran through almost every talk this week, even when the speakers didn't say it outright: when building gets cheap, judgment gets expensive.

Joe Futty from Pipedrive said it directly. When you can build almost anything, the hard part stops being engineering capacity and becomes judgment: deciding which of the things you could build is worth building at all. Krasi Bozhinkova made the same point from another angle. Speed is now a commodity, so it can't be your strategy. Good judgment plus speed is great. Confusion plus speed is just faster confusion.

I find this oddly reassuring. The part of product work that's hardest to automate is the part that was always the real job: understanding the problem well enough to know which option is the right one.

One habit I'm taking home, from John Moriarty's talk: build a deliberate pause into fast projects. Halfway through, stop and ask "are we still solving the right problem?" When the work moves quickly, that question is the easiest one to skip and the most expensive one to miss.

## Post 4 — Stop saying "discovery", start saying "money"

The same idea kept coming up this week: why solid product work so often fails to convince the people who hold the budget.

Usually the work is solid. What fails is the language we wrap it in. When we say "discovery", "tech debt" or "backlog", the people deciding budgets hear excuses for not shipping what they asked for.

Dave Martin, who coaches neurodiverse leaders, shared the clearest example. One of his clients stopped pitching "product discovery" to the board and called it "due diligence before committing budget" instead. Same activity, language a board already trusts, and it won immediate buy-in. Martin Eriksson made the same point in his session: drop "discovery", talk about risk reduction, because executives understand risk.

Dave had a second move worth stealing: aim for a phrase people will repeat once you've left the room. Another client reduced a pitch to "minutes, not months", and it turned up in documents he was never part of.

Rich Mironov went furthest, suggesting a money figure on every roadmap item. Executives ignore the boxes and read the numbers, and the conversation becomes about value instead of process.

I've spent years explaining the how of my work and wondering why it left people cold. What I'm taking home is to lead with what the work is worth to them, in their words, and let them ask for the detail.

## Post 5 — Use AI to understand, not to skip understanding

The most useful thing I heard this week came from someone who isn't in product at all. Brian Cox, the physicist, was asked about AI in education.

He said using AI to learn is fine. The risk is using it to answer questions you should be sitting with, so you never internalise anything. The goal is to deepen your own understanding rather than to collect the answer. He uses AI as a patient tutor, one with no ego and no impatience, to explore an idea from several angles until he understands it himself.

He also said being corrected is how you make progress. He once got corrected on live BBC radio by another physicist. They went to dinner afterwards and became friends.

I notice I sometimes reach for AI to get past a problem rather than to understand it. The two feel similar in the moment and produce very different people over a year.

So this is what I'm trying: when I ask AI to explain something, I ask it to show me how it got there, then I put it in my own words before moving on. If I can't, I didn't understand it yet.

## Post 6 — Christian Idiodi: when did you last have a great day at work?

Christian Idiodi closed his mtpcon talk with a simple question: when was the last time you had a great day at work?

He meant the kind of day you'd happily relive. For most product people, he said, that memory is a release that delighted someone, or a problem you cracked together that changed how things worked. It's the last time you felt like a creator, rather than someone managing a backlog. That, he said, is where the discipline is heading: you become "the conductor of capability, not the author of backlogs".

It meant something different to me, because I've already answered that question by leaving. A while ago I left a corporate role to build something of my own, just the two of us. Part of the reason was that the great days had become rare, buried under process and meetings about meetings.

I don't think you have to quit to get those days back. But the question is worth sitting with honestly. If you can't remember your last great day at work, that's information worth taking seriously. It's telling you something about where your time is going.

When was yours?

## Post 7 — April Dunford: half your deals are lost to "no decision"

April Dunford's talk at mtpcon gave me one of the most interesting statistics I heard all week: roughly half of B2B deals now end in no decision. The buyer doesn't choose a competitor, they choose no one at all.

Her explanation: in a market where every product says "AI-powered", buyers are scared and confused, and everything looks the same. When you can't tell the options apart, the safest move is to do nothing.

Her fix is a clear point of view on the future: a real position on where your market is heading, the kind that explains why your roadmap looks the way it does. She pointed to companies making different bets, Anthropic, OpenAI, Replit and Salesforce, each one aimed at a different future. A buyer can choose between those. They can't choose between five products that describe themselves in identical words.

I took this personally, because I run a small product company now, and it's tempting to describe what we do in the same language everyone else uses. Sounding like the category feels safe, but it's the quickest way to vanish into it.

So this is the question I'm taking home: if a customer put our product next to three others and they all sounded the same, why would they pick us? And could I answer that in one sentence?
