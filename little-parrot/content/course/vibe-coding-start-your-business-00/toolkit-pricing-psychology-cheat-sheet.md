## Pricing Psychology Cheat Sheet

A quick reference for the pricing psychology techniques from Challenge 7. Open this when you're designing your pricing page.

### Anchoring

A higher-priced option makes a lower-priced option feel more reasonable by comparison.

**How to use it:** Show a Pro plan ($15/month) next to a Starter plan ($9/month). The $9 feels like a sensible entry point. Without the Pro plan, visitors have nothing to compare to.

### Decoy pricing

Add an option you don't expect anyone to buy. Its job is to make your target option look better by comparison.

**How to use it:** Add a plan that's priced close to your target plan but offers less. For example: Basic at $9, Premium at $14 (with fewer features than Pro), and Pro at $15. Nobody picks Premium, but next to it, Pro looks like an obvious deal for just $1 more. The decoy shifts what people are willing to pay by changing what they compare against.

### Endowment effect

People value something more once they feel it's theirs, even if the ownership is temporary or just perceived.

**How to use it:** Let users try your product through a free trial so it starts to feel like theirs. Encourage early customisation (themes, profiles, workspace names) so they invest in it. When describing your paid plan, frame features as something they already have ("Your plan includes...") rather than something they need to buy.

### Pricing page checklist

When designing your pricing page, check:

- [ ] There are 2-3 tiers (not more)
- [ ] Each tier clearly states what's included
- [ ] There's an option to try the product so users feel ownership before paying
- [ ] Users can personalise early (profile, workspace name, settings)
