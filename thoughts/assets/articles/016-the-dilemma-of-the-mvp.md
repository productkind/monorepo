# The Dilemma of the MVP

*What exactly should you build as your first iteration of a valuable product?*

**Date:** September 24, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

When we started building Little Parrot, a micro-learning platform for busy product people, it was actually harder than I thought to agree on a minimum viable product (MVP).

I've been in the tech industry for over 10 years, working in a variety of roles: account management, development, product. I've worked at both B2B and B2C companies, big and small.

Previously, I successfully carved out MVPs for replacing legacy systems or adding a new feature to a live product. But with Little Parrot, I found myself second-guessing when we were trying to define an MVP to build and test our idea.

In this article I want to untangle why that was, and explore the dilemma of the MVP.

## The Logic of an MVP

When we build a new product, there are a ton of assumptions we make. At its core, we believe it will be valuable for someone, so they will pay for it.

We are told to build fast and iterate, based on user feedback to ensure we don't build something for a long time that ends up not solving a user's need or problem, or fulfilling a desire. Therefore, we aim to build the smallest possible piece that can deliver core user value.

And user value is crucial, because if your potential user tries your product, and it doesn't prove to be valuable enough, they just abandon it, and might never come back.

So this smallest possible piece needs to be incredibly valuable and preferably frictionless, since getting the attention of your target audience is also quite hard at this early stage – unless you've already exited from building Twitter or Slack and have a huge network to amplify your message.

Well, that isn't the case for us. We're still in the validation phase and we are grateful for each website visitor who enables us to learn something new. They help us to test our hypotheses and see if Little Parrot is valuable for product people.

## The Contradiction of "Building Fast" and "Valuable and Frictionless"

When we talk about building fast, it's easy to assume the first version of our product can be wonky and full of errors, as long as we get it into users' hands quickly. You might even remember Mark Zuckerberg's famous saying of "move fast and break things." But "building fast" refers to the scope, not the quality of your product.

You might give your wonky and error-prone product to your trusted friends to give feedback on. Or to a group of alpha users whose expectations about the product quality is managed. But shipping something with a bad user experience to the wider public will result in lost users.

In Little Parrot's case, we were concerned, if our initial offering was hard to try and didn't work as our users expect it, then we'll wrongly conclude that there's no market need for it. When, in reality, our execution was the issue.

"Building fast" means that you should slice out the smallest possible piece of your product that can deliver core value without building all the bells and whistles of it. And the "fast" part specifically refers to the speed of getting this limited scope into users' hands to validate whether it's valuable for them.

"Valuable and frictionless" is about the experience of that limited scope. This is where quality comes in. If the small piece you build quickly isn't valuable, users won't stick around long enough for you to get meaningful feedback.

If it's not frictionless or is full of bugs, users will get frustrated and leave. This leads to skewed validation results. We wouldn't know if they left because the content of our micro-course wasn't good or because the experience in the course flow was painful.

## Our Dilemma

We started Little Parrot by building out the landing page, then the first micro-course, and a pledge flow to test payment intent. When we had half of a micro-course ready, we wanted to get feedback on the course content. But we were wondering, does this really solve a problem or provide useful insight for our target audience, busy product people? Does it deliver on our promise of being "science-based" and "practical?"

Even if it's just half a micro-course, it needs to leave the user feeling like they gained something significant and that their time was well spent. If it's not valuable, they won't come back for the next half or the next course.

Then we had to consider the frictionless part. Is the experience of accessing, consuming, and understanding that content smooth? Are there any technical glitches, confusing navigation, or unnecessary steps that would make a busy product person abandon it? Friction kills engagement, especially for an audience with limited time.

Since we were part of Lovable Shipped – a competition with the goal to build something from scratch and ship it – we had access to a community to test and give feedback on our product.

## Iterate

Iteration builds on a solid core. Once you have that small, valuable, and frictionless core, you can then iterate.

First, you gather feedback on that core experience to learn about what worked, what your users liked, and what was missing, what they wished for.

Then, you build the next small, valuable, and frictionless piece based on that learning. For us, it was the second half of the course. But it could have been a new feature that might be useful in the learning experience, or a new course on a different topic.

So, for our microlearning platform, we had to make that initial half micro-course an impactful and seamless experience. That's how we'd get the engagement and feedback necessary to guide our iterations forward.

We learned some really valuable feedback:

- Our initial users thought that the focus on microlearning and real-world prompting use cases felt highly relevant.
- People wanted to try the course before buying it, so we made the first challenge free.
- Showing too many steps overwhelmed users, so we broke the course down into challenges and displayed the steps for each specific challenge. This way our users can take a bite-sized learning challenge while their coffee brews, and come back later.
- Users wanted to see how the course looked like even before clicking away from the homepage. That's why we added a gif to the hero section to display a couple of steps of the course.

## The Takeaway

The key here is to define the absolute minimum you need to build to deliver a complete, valuable, and frictionless experience for one specific use case. This is how you make your MVP good enough to start getting meaningful feedback, even if it's not the full vision.
