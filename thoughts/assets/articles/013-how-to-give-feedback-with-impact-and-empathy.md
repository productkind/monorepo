# How to Give Feedback with Impact and Empathy

*Practical techniques to make your feedback easier to give and to hear*

**Date:** September 3, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

If you've been reading along, you might know by now that I have some very strong perfectionist tendencies and very high standards for myself and people around me.

So when I discovered that a junior product manager I was onboarding to our team kept making typos in our user stories, I got that feeling you get from a mosquito bite – you desperately want to scratch it, but you know it's a bad idea.

I had this strong urge to sneak in and correct them. But then, you know, modern project management tools track who last edited an issue. So I couldn't just keep overwriting the stories he created.

I felt a bit trapped, because I knew that this was a stickler move. Others might not even have noticed the typos, let alone been bothered by them.

I was lucky to have a leadership coach at the time, so I brought this issue to our session, as it kept annoying me.

The coach highlighted that this issue is mine, it doesn't have anything to do with impact. Cough… Doesn't it?! Cough. It might hinder understandability, but I let it slide.

"Listen, I have a problem with typos, do you mind if I correct them all?" she suggested I ask the junior PM. This way, I wasn't overcorrecting, it was something I was doing for me.

She advised adding a way for the junior PM to change his mind by saying, "If it's bothering you in the future, I'll stop."

"When it's your thing, tell people it's yours and ask people if you can correct them," she continued, "it has to do with your level of comfort."

So true. When we give feedback, we have to assess the reason we are giving that feedback. Is it for you, for me, or for someone else?

## Steps of Giving Feedback

Okay, before you think I just helicoptered above this junior product manager, I also wanted to give him constructive feedback that could actually help him to facilitate meetings more effectively.

Giving negative feedback is so hard. We want to be nice, we want others to like us – I certainly do. But without giving feedback about a behaviour that has a negative impact, nothing will change.

From the other side, receiving feedback is a gift, it's a learning opportunity. Even though, sometimes we don't take it that way. We get defensive, we close off, and often don't acknowledge the truth in the critique.

I think both sides are especially difficult, because we're not taught to give feedback in a productive way. We get personal, we criticise, we judge.

So I tried to follow the technique by the book.

### Focus on the Behaviour

Give feedback on the behaviour, not the person's character. It's essential that you describe the situation, the action you saw or heard, instead of adding your interpretation to it.

Also, try to give feedback close to when it happened, so you both remember the details. The closer feedback comes to the actual behaviour, the stronger the learning connection is.

After our planning session, I pulled him aside. It's always best to give feedback one-on-one, rather than in front of the team, so the person doesn't feel ostracised.

"Brian," I started, "do you mind if I give you some feedback on the meeting facilitation today?"

"Um, sure," he said hesitantly.

"In the planning today," I continued, "you opened the first user story you wrote, and asked, 'Clear?' Then nobody responded, so you just kept going to the next one."

It's best to avoid generalising and using phrases like "always," or "never." Just stick to the facts when you describe a behaviour. Otherwise, you risk getting into an argument, because the person might highlight the one time when they've done the thing differently.

### Share the Impact

Describing the impact highlights the relevance of the feedback. It helps with understanding why changing this behaviour matters, and creates a sense of responsibility, such as "My behaviour directly affects team clarity and efficiency." It links the behaviour to outcomes and might motivate the person to improve.

So I tried to describe the impact as clearly as possible, "I saw that people quieted down, and nobody responded, so we might have missed important questions that later can slow the team down."

Don't use "you" phrases, like "You made them pause." That's just an opinion, and it's subjective. He could reply, "No, I didn't." Then we could just bicker about it.

If I were to say, "You were too firm, and closed off the conversation," it can feel like a judgement on his character and might trigger defensiveness that can prevent him from learning.

### Understand Their Perspective

The tricky part is that we assign our own meaning to the behaviours we observe. We make assumptions based on our interpretations, and then we act based on those assumptions.

For instance, he said "Clear?" very firmly and – in my opinion – too directly, as the user story should already be clear for the team. And this is key, it was my interpretation of the situation, not the universal truth. Even though the reaction of the team made me think they didn't appreciate the tone, and it didn't invite conversation, it's still not an objective observation.

In product management, we learned that making assumptions and not validating them is an unwise thing to do. So we should remember this in these tricky, feedback inducing situations.

Instead of making all these assumptions, you might want to be curious, and ask questions through an honest, open and friendly dialogue. Instead of asking, "Why did you do that?" invite reflection.

So I asked him, "How did you see this situation? What were your thoughts?"

Then just listen and acknowledge whatever the person says, by saying "I hear you," so they feel validated. It doesn't mean you agree with them.

### Facilitate Options

Social feedback, particularly criticism, activates threat detection systems in the brain. This creates a fundamental tension: while feedback is essential for learning, negative feedback can trigger defensive responses that interfere with the very learning process it's meant to facilitate.

It's likely you'll get into a disagreement if you try to correct a behaviour. People tend to choose something that's adequate to their style, so it's better not to give corrective input on behavioural feedback.

Therefore, offering options or asking reflective questions works better than prescribing behaviour. What you can do is facilitate a conversation and, in doing so, create a learning experience with questions like, "What could we do differently next time?"

I asked him, "Looking ahead, what are some other ways you could check for understanding?"

"Huh, maybe just wait until they reply," he said.

I didn't give him my opinion, just prompted him, "Okay, what else?"

You can do this, until they give you a good option. Then lock that in, "That sounds good."

At the end, I asked him, "Could I give you my opinion?" If you'd like to share a practice you've seen working, you can do the same.

I suggested, "Next time, you could try reading out the user story, so everyone can take the time to understand it. Then ask the team 'What questions do you have?' to encourage more discussion."

He started nodding, "Okay, good idea."

## Go On, Give Your Feedback

I'm impatient, and I found that it can make it hard for me to get into the other person's perspective when they make typos or facilitate a meeting inefficiently.

A thought-provoking podcast episode between Brené Brown and Angela Duckworth recently reminded me that "empathy is an antidote to impatience."

So I should remember that person in front of me I'm giving feedback to is not me. It doesn't matter how much typos irritate me, or how good facilitation looks for me. I should stay curious, find out their perspective on these topics, and how they want to get feedback.

This junior PM certainly didn't turn into a master facilitator after this one feedback, but I'd like to think it helped him improve. Often, you have to keep giving pointers for the other person to support them grow. Just make sure they're constructive, specific, and timely.

And in case you'd like notes on how you could improve, you can ask your team at the end of your next meeting, "What are two things I could do better in future meetings?"
