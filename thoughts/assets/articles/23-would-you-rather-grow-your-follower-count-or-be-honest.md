I'm conflicted between these two opposing approaches to posting content online:

You project success, show the good bits, make everything look very aspirational.

You are honest, show your meagre follower count, and recount all the things you've tried that failed to significantly increase your audience.

I don't want to deceive people. I also don't want to produce more "overnight success" content and pose as someone who has "made it" – whatever that means.

At the same time, everywhere I look, I see the "5 things that will solve all your problems" lists with millions of views, and the "here's the formula for success" carousels.

On paper, I might seem successful, but often I don't feel like it.

The definition of "success" I use here is "you are a loaded business person who has figured out how to comfortably live off their skills."

Of course, there is more to success than that. But I can still feel unimpressed with myself, even if I'm writing these lines from a cool Estonian bookshop-café, without being monitored by a manager.

## Why is it so hard to be satisfied with what we've got?

It's the constant comparison. You know that experiment where two monkeys were getting cucumbers for the same task they successfully completed, and they were perfectly content. Then the scientists started to give one of the monkeys sweet grapes, instead of the watery cucumber for the same task, and the cucumber-fed primate lashed out. It was perfectly satisfied with the cucumber until its counterpart got a more delicious treat.

In an episode of Lenny's podcast, a billionaire said that there will always be another billionaire with a bigger super yacht, and that's why so many of them are actually miserable. It sounds ridiculous, doesn't it? I'd think that a billionaire's biggest problem is making sure their money is invested into something safe, so they can just comfortably live off the interest. But it's human nature (and monkeys' too).

So, what can you do? What can I do to appreciate my situation?

Stop scrolling Instagram, LinkedIn, and just get back to work. Or go outside, nature is a surprisingly lovely antidote to all this nonsense.

Well, I did take a walk, and actually got some clarity. In the messiness of all the emotions I've had, I forgot to ask myself, "Why am I doing all of this: posting on LinkedIn, writing articles, or even creating courses for Little Parrot?" I want to help women benefit more from technology.

So, the question is then, "How can I do that better?" I think presenting an honest picture of what building a business is really like is more helpful, than sharing only the shiny façade.

I know that I rebel against the hype and guru positioning, and I don't want to put more anxiety into the world. So, maybe it can help someone to know that I do doubt sometimes if we are going to achieve a bigger positive impact.

I'm curious, what do you enjoy reading more: people's advice who have figured out how to live from their passion, or honest accounts of not succeeding?
