# Stop Defining Yourself by Your Title: How to Build a Resilient Identity

*Why anchoring your sense of self to your values helps you navigate career shifts and uncertainty.*

**Date:** October 22, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

I was sitting around a beautiful table in the Tuscan countryside, chatting with the other retreat attendees. I was already somewhat of an outlier, because every other attendee was a self-proclaimed writer. They weren't all published authors, but all of them had their writing projects: a memoir, a novel, poems. And most of them had their own Substack newsletter.

At the time, I was just considering starting this newsletter, but repeatedly convinced myself that no one wanted to hear from me. And anyway, there are far more interesting voices in product.

"I'm a product manager in the tech industry," I said. 'Product manager' is often a title that folks outside the tech industry – and sometimes within it – have no clue about. Software developer, yes, they write code. Designer, of course, they design screens and experiences. But product manager? They surely must be managing something. It's probably important, because they have 'manager' in their title.

I had a hard time explaining more, because I wasn't working for a company anymore. I left my full-time job the year before. I was still experimenting. There wasn't a clear-cut label I could apply to myself.

My identity was built around what I did. So, was I still a product manager, even though I wasn't actively managing a product?

## The Identity Crisis: What Happens When the Label Disappears?

We have a tendency to define ourselves narrowly by roles or achievements. But these attachments make our identities fragile when circumstances change.

Dr. Maya Shankar, a cognitive scientist, really helped me to rethink how to build a more resilient identity that's not rooted in my job title. In her podcast, A Slight Change of Plans, she said:

> "Rather than attaching your identity to specific pursuits, like being a mum or a writer or a podcaster, it can be better to anchor your identity to the underlying features of those pursuits that really make you light up, that really make you tick. It's like, can I anchor my value and my worth and my sense of self not to what I do, but why I do it? Because that is the part of us that nothing in the universe can touch. It's well protected forever, and then it helps guide you in your next pursuit."

The problem was that I defined myself by what I was doing, instead of why I was doing it. As Dr. Shankar says, when we define ourselves by our underlying why (values, motivation, purpose), rather than our what (job titles or roles), our identity becomes more resilient.

We can then use this as our North Star when we're facing inflection points in our life. Change happens when the roles, labels, or life structures we've tied our sense of self to are taken away or no longer fit our reality. Just as when I decided to leave my full-time job and started trying different things, my reality changed. I couldn't define myself anymore by what I was doing, because it was many different things. It wasn't a clear-cut title.

Even though in such circumstances we tend to refer to this feeling as being stripped of our identity, and change can feel like a contraction, it's actually often a space of expansion. We can discover new capabilities and sources of meaning we might not have explored before. A kind of deep personal transformation happens in these cases.

## Values as Your North Star

Raj Bhargava has a similar view on this. He teaches the Your Next Life Chapter class at Stanford and also founded a self-paced coaching app with a personalised AI companion called Your Next Work Chapter. In his course and app, he combines behavioural science and design thinking for purpose-driven career guidance.

I've had a few conversations with him, and he advocates for defining our values, which will inform our purpose and identity. It takes time to figure out our purpose; we need to give ourselves some space to experiment.

When I asked him about his journey from a seasoned tech entrepreneur in Silicon Valley to teaching and helping people find their purpose, he replied, "It took me three years to figure out my purpose for this chapter of my life." He explored and learned different things during that time, and suggested that "you will know when you arrive at your chapter that feels just right." He advised me to "centre your identity on your values."

So, we can have different chapters in our lives, we change, and that's perfectly normal.

## Moving from 'What' I'm Doing to 'Why' I'm Doing It

Through these knowledgeable people, I learned that the loss of old identities is often the precondition for authentic growth. It's in the dismantling of who we thought we were that we uncover who we can become.

So I asked myself, "What are the underlying features of what I used to do?"

In product management, we are connecting with people – users, developers, designers, other stakeholders – and also solving problems. Our goal is to create value, both for our organisation and our end-users. And personally, my goal has always been to do something purposeful through understanding why we do what we do.

I discovered that my desire to connect emotionally with people has been at the heart of my role and why I chose to build products. And, besides that, doing this in an intellectually stimulating environment with kind people is very important for me.

## Product Manager is a State of Mind

Last week, I was co-organising a ProductTank meetup. We were in the lucky position to have Bruce McCarthy as one of our speakers. Afterwards, we chatted in a circle, and he said, "Product manager is a state of mind, it's a way of thinking."

Even after he became a consultant and coach, he still referred to himself as a product manager. "That's so true," I thought. Questioning the status quo, focusing on creating value, collaborating with diverse people, and being curious – all these are traits we can find new ways to express when old roles fade.

Product manager is my strongest professional identity too, precisely because of the way of thinking it represents. What's yours?
