# How to Lead Without Authority: A Product Manager's Guide

*What to do when you are not the boss, but still have to lead*

**Date:** August 27, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

Leadership is often imagined in a narrow way. People picture someone – usually a man – with all the right answers, an unshakable vision, and having the final say.

But when I think of true leaders, I think of Jacinda Ardern, the former Prime Minister of New Zealand. She led her country through a pandemic with some of the world's lowest death rates and introduced a national focus on wellbeing, not just GDP.

I also think of Melinda French Gates, who made gender equality a priority at the world's largest private foundation. Now she focuses on accelerating social progress, and advancing women's power and influence through her company, Pivotal Ventures.

I also think of Malala Yousafzai, the co-founder of the Malala Fund and the youngest-ever Nobel Peace Prize laureate for advocating for every girl to have access to education.

And I think of Shonda Rhimes, who built the storytelling company Shondaland and changed television by pioneering a new standard for diversity on screen through shows like Grey's Anatomy, Scandal, and How to get away with murder.

What connects these leaders is that they lead with empathy and advocate for people-centred solutions. They are complex human-beings who don't pretend to know everything. They admit when they're wrong and show that they value failure as a learning opportunity.

Of course, you don't need to be a prime minister or a foundation head to be a leader. Being a leader doesn't depend on your title, but on your actions.

> "We have this notion that confidence in leadership comes through having all the answers all the time; we are trusted because we show no shred of doubt. But I think, in any crisis, trust is built by being open about information and knowledge gaps."
> – Jacinda Ardern, Source: Harvard Business Review

## Product Manager as a Leader

As a product manager, you aren't the line manager of anyone in your product team, but you are leading the team. Your teammates look to you to set direction, create structure, clearly communicate decisions, represent the team's viewpoints with stakeholders, and ensure you're building something valuable for your users.

You're supposed to give constructive feedback to teammates, stakeholders, and even higher-ups. You're expected to motivate your team, and ensure they are performing well. You're the one who shares bad news or difficult business decisions and then handles the aftermath so the team doesn't fall apart.

And you do all of this without authority over anyone's salary, promotion, or professional development.

So how do you do all this? How do you lead without authority?

## Building Blocks for Leading Without Authority

A couple of years ago, I joined a company where there were no product teams. There was a group of product managers, developers, and designers, but they were working in a more ad hoc way. The company had started to insource development a year before I joined, but was still working with external development agencies too.

I was supposed to improve the e-commerce flow and prepare it for a new offering. It was built on legacy third-party technology, an external development team was needed for major changes, there were performance issues, buggy behaviour, and various other problems. Each stakeholder group (marketing, finance, sales, development, etc.) had a different viewpoint of what "improving" this meant.

I had to ask for "resources" from the Head of Development to get started.

### Form a Product Team

Having a team where you can start building a good culture, trust and psychological safety is essential when building a product. If you work with people from different organisational units, they could have different priorities. This makes it harder to create a sense of ownership for the product you're building than if you're all part of the same team.

I knew that I needed the following skills on the team: backend and frontend development, infrastructure, and design. So, by talking to the Head of Development, I managed to get the right people. Some weren't allocated full-time to the e-commerce flow, but I still made them part of our team. I didn't just want to assign Jira tickets to them. I wanted to involve them in discovery and figure out the right solution together.

One of the first activities I organised for this group of people was a workshop to officially form our team. There isn't one way to work together as a team. You need to identify what each person's expectations are, agree on working methodology and discuss the details of how you'll collaborate.

### Create Processes That Serve Your Team

"It was very humanistic, but not bad," one of the developers told me after the team formation workshop. He referred to the part where I asked them about what they valued when building a product. Apparently, no one asked him that before.

Being curious about people's needs and preferences is key to building a strong team. And you can create processes to support them.

Processes are tools, not goals. They should serve the team, not the other way around. Ask yourselves: What does this process do for our team? Introduce them only if they help collaboration or clarity, and avoid adding ceremonies just to tick boxes.

As you start working together, revisit if a ceremony you agreed on still makes sense. Sometimes it takes trying something to identify it's not serving the team, or not actually needed.

### Be Knowledgable in Your Field

For your team to trust and respect you, you have to be knowledgeable in your field. You have to communicate clearly, and understand your business, customers, and the craft of building products well.

So I started by mapping out the ecosystem that connected to and made up the e-commerce flow. This helped me to understand how the 14 different systems related to one another, an understanding that was crucial for making product decisions later on.

### Create a Blameless Culture

When you're building software, it's inevitable that things go wrong: production breaks, bugs surface, new features take twice as much time to build than expected. In these cases, you need to make sure that no one in your team is blamed. You work together, it's all your shared responsibility.

I've recently heard a young man say he didn't like playing team sports. After some follow-up questions, he revealed that he didn't like playing football because "when you make mistakes, your whole team gets angry at you. And when you make a mistake in an individual sport, it's only you who fails."

I felt sorry for him. Obviously, his team didn't have a good and supportive culture. And without that, it's impossible to perform well. In order to innovate you need to take risks. Those risks sometimes turn into gold, but sometimes you fall on your face. And when the falling happens, you need an encouraging and caring team who catches you, otherwise you won't take any risks again.

#### So, How Can You Create a Nourishing Culture?

Start by admitting when you make a mistake. There's no better way to show your team it's okay to mess up than owning your mistake when you make one. It can be hard to admit you did something wrong, but taking responsibility for it will signal to your team that you're a solid leader. Product work is not about perfection, it's about collaboration and resilience.

When someone else in your team makes a mistake, do try to understand why and how it happened. Make sure to do this in a blameless way. It can help if you use "we" language, rather than singling someone out. A good team culture is not about sweeping mistakes under a rug, but openly discussing them to learn something new. And that's valuable, because next time you'll do better.

Since I've been working in product, it happened several times that my team couldn't deliver something as fast as the stakeholders expected. It proved to be more complex or something unexpected happened. In these cases, I always tried to understand from the developers on my team what caused the delay, and then I translated that technical jargon into a language a business stakeholder could easily understand.

Whenever I delivered the news, I always said that we had faced some unexpected difficulties that resulted in a longer development time. Don't let anyone hang out to dry. Don't single out a developer and say "Matt realised that the implementation is more complex than he expected." You are a team working on a product and if one fails, all fail; if one succeeds, all succeed.

The work is often complicated and tough, but a nourishing team culture can make it easier to navigate through the challenges.

### Encourage Ownership

During our first discussions with my developer colleagues, I quickly realised that some of their expectations were simply to "tell me what to do and leave me alone to work," which for them meant writing code. This was the opposite of ownership.

Their approach was understandable, though. They had worked for a long time at a software agency, following a "handoff process:" they received long specifications, built what the document contained (or a version of it), and then handed it over to the client.

I don't think this is the ideal way of building products, since it omits true collaboration. I needed their technical insights earlier in the process to come up with the best possible solutions together.

As a leader, it's easy to feel the urge to take charge of everything, even though you're not the expert in every topic. So, the wise thing to say is "I don't know, what do you think?" Then listen and learn from your teammates.

Leaders so often think that their job is to always know the right answer. But real leaders are curious, so they admit what they don't know, ask, learn, and reflect.

I showed my team I valued their perspective and counted on their expertise. In return, they took ownership of the work.

### Encourage Productive Disagreements

Healthy conflict requires trust. Disagreements surface better solutions, but only if people feel safe enough to voice them. Your role is to make sure debates stay about ideas, not egos.

Many organisations still reward people who have a strong opinion, instead of rewarding curiosity and openness to learn.

### Celebrate Successes

Take time to celebrate successes your team achieved – small or big. Recognition builds team cohesion, elevates moral and motivation.

I found that bringing cakes to celebrate a significant release makes the team feel appreciated.

### Make Space for Every Voice

Great ideas don't always come from the loudest people. Sometimes, in a team discussion the ideas of more introverted people don't surface, because when they summon the courage to share, someone cuts in. Then they cannot find the right time to jump in, and eventually the conversation moves on.

As a leader, it's your responsibility to monitor the room during team discussions and make sure everyone gets a chance to add their input.

If someone is silent for a long time, ask them, "Maria, what do you think about this approach?" Be intentional that everyone's voice and ideas are heard.

## Closing Thought

Leading without authority is hard, but it's also one of the most rewarding parts of being a product manager.

Your power doesn't come from the ability to give a pay rise or promotion. It comes from building a foundation of trust, creating a space where people feel safe and heard, sharing ownership, and making space for people to do their best work.

Authority and title don't make you a leader. People choosing to follow you does.
