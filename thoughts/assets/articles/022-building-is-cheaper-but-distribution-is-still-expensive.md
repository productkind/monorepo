# 🦦 Building is cheap(er), but distribution is still expensive

> Why the cost of building software is not the full story to building a successful product

**By Kinga Magyar**

---

You've probably heard this idea that building is cheap now, so every company is producing more. More lines of code, more features, more products.

But **what's the point** of all this stuff?

When it comes to a new product, it might be easier and cheaper to build it, but the expensive part is still figuring out the right distribution channels. When I say "distribution", I simply mean how our target audience finds what we've built.

Building a product is like 25% of the job. The other 75% looks a bit like this:

1. You need to make sure it **solves a real problem** for your target users to make it valuable.
2. You need a lot of people with that specific problem to find it. That's **distribution**.
3. Your target audience needs to have time to try it, and find it useful enough to come back to it over and over. So, you also need to drive the **behaviour change** required for them to adopt the product.
4. And finally, the holy grail of a business: **revenue**. You need to make sure this product is so valuable that your users will happily pay for it.

For a B2C product, getting to the point where you have enough paying users to comfortably live off the income takes a lot of time and experimentation. Unless, of course, you're a "tech bro" with a massive network of influential people ready to amplify your launch, meaning you don't have to figure out scrappy ways to reach your audience.

We aren't in that camp. With Little Parrot, we've validated that we're building something that solves a real problem, but the bit we haven't figured out yet is distribution. What are the channels that will bring in new users in a sustained way?

We intentionally avoided paid marketing so far. Instead of throwing money at the problem, which might not even yield results, we want to validate which organic channels actually work for us first, and that our messaging resonates.

## Our experiments

We've been running a couple of experiments to identify where our target audience hangs out and where we should focus our energy.

### Instagram

Instagram was one of the channels that we experimented with last year. We consistently posted carousels featuring product management cartoons. What we learned was that static images and carousels aren't the best medium for reaching people who don't follow you yet.

Our new hypothesis is that videos, specifically reels, is the way forward. So this is what we are testing next. We've already seen that reels get way more views from non-follower, than carousels.

I recently read that if you add music to a carousel, Instagram treats it similarly to a reel, giving it a much bigger reach than a completely silent post. Based on our experiments so far, this hasn't been true for us. Two carousels we published this way still only reached people already following us.

I'll report back when we have more definitive results.

### LinkedIn

The other week, I was at an event where I chatted with one of the initiators of EU-INC, a grassroots campaign for a standard startup incorporation form in the EU that was recently presented to the European Commission. Their petition was signed and supported by almost every famous startup founder in Europe (from Stripe and Wise to Lovable and ElevenLabs). Naturally, I was curious: how did a 10-person team manage to rally such massive public support for a policy proposal? What was their main distribution channel?

The answer was LinkedIn. One side of me was glad to hear this, because we all have access to LinkedIn. It feels empowering to know we have agency. The other side of me was a bit annoyed. Posting on LinkedIn feels like a chore. I hate telling people, "Look at me, look what great things I'm making!"

Even though I try to reframe it and follow my rule of only posting stuff on LinkedIn that might be helpful for someone else – a thought-provoking concept, or a tool they could use – it's still hard. I think the underlying issue is something that many women are taught from an early age: be small, be silent, don't bother others with your opinion. It enrages me, but I still feel that subconscious reluctance to share and "bother" people on LinkedIn.

Writing articles is one thing. If you are not interested, then you won't read it. But a LinkedIn post pops up on your feed without you opting in.

Well, actually, I've just realised you have opted in. You have connected with me or followed me, so you have to see what I'm up to. Hm, writing really does help with thinking. All those people who say "don't outsource your thinking to AI" have a point.

All this to say: LinkedIn is an important distribution platform (unfortunately). For Little Parrot, it's been the strongest acquisition channel so far.

### Conferences

The problem with conferences is that your activity there doesn't scale. At the same time, when starting to build your business, you need to do things that don't scale. The founders of Airbnb famously went door to door in the early days to convince people to sign up to their platform. The idea is that once you have enough users, the referral flywheel starts moving.

Last year, we gave talks and workshops at five conferences. At the time, we had only one course on Little Parrot, and at some conferences the audience wasn't the right target for us. But what we learned was that conferences are a great way to build awareness about your product.

### Communities

Posting in different communities is tricky, because you don't want to just "sell" your product. You want to offer value to the members. I suppose this is true for every channel, even if your product aims to help them, you need to give something extra upfront.

If you find the right community, it can be a great acquisition channel. The key is to be razor-sharp with your targeting.

For us, we're building Little Parrot with women in mind who can't code yet, they might be founders, freelancers, or employees at a company. What connects them is an interest in building digital products, whether as a core business, a tool to support their business (like a lead magnet), or to communicate ideas at work.

The other thread we've started to develop more courses around is using AI to save time. We aren't really interested in the "10x product manager" narrative, we just want to enable people to do their work more quickly so they can spend time on activities they enjoy.

So, those are the communities we're focusing on, trying to give them value alongside letting them know our AI and vibe coding courses exist.

## Accelerate learning, not delivery speed

The right metric isn't lines of code produced (it never was), or number of new products shipped, but value created that translates to revenue.

Of course, value creation is a notoriously hard thing to measure. A good proxy metric is how much you learn along the way. This helps you figure out what works and what doesn't. Because value creation is an iterative process, you need to reflect on the outcomes of your activities to improve and get closer to making a positive impact.

The current narrative tells us that because AI makes building digital products so cheap, we can just test everything live in production. But constantly changing the UI creates a heavy mental load for your users (I'm looking at you, Lovable!). Plus, if you create a mountain of output with AI but your token costs end up sky-high, you haven't built a sustainable business. You probably should have run smaller, cheaper experiments first, rather than producing stuff that not enough people are willing to pay for.

At the end of the day, our goal shouldn't be to use AI to speed up delivery just for the sake of more output. The real superpower of AI is its ability to accelerate our learning speed. Build less, learn faster, and focus on the part that is crucial: finding your people.
