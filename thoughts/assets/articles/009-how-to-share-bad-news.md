# How to Share Bad News

*A proven technique for sharing difficult news with your team*

**Date:** August 6, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

A couple of years ago, I was sitting in a meeting room in Berlin.

> "We might need to sunset the product. It's a question of costs, and the board will decide," one of our business stakeholders was explaining.

"When will they decide?" I asked, a sense of unease starting to form.

"We don't know yet, but probably around the end of the quarter."

"But we still need to deliver on our quarterly commitments," our other business stakeholder interjected.

"But that's over a month away," I thought, the frustration building. "That's too long to leave the team in this state of uncertainty. How am I going to tell them?"

I dwelt on this situation for the rest of my visit that week. It was a really tough spot to be in, and I felt the weight of the responsibility on my shoulders. How could I expect people to stay motivated and continue building something when it might all just go to waste?

At the time, I was in the lucky position to have a leadership coach through my work. So, when I got back to Lisbon, I had a session with her.

We're not taught how to deliver bad news, even though it's an unfortunate but inevitable part of our lives. In my coaching session, I got straight to the point.

"I'd like to change what we planned to focus on in our previous session. Something happened last week, and I'd like your advice on it." I went on to explain the situation to her.

"You want the psychological side of giving bad news," she concluded.

"Exactly!" I replied. I loved that she had a science-backed perspective.

Now, if you've ever had a coaching session, you might be raising your eyebrows. You might be thinking, "Getting advice from your coach? That's not their job." And you'd be right. Coaches who subscribe to the pure coaching model don't give suggestions, but ask questions to help you find your own solution. But luckily, my coach was open to share her vast experience with me so I could tap into her wisdom. Honestly, I found this type of coaching more effective for me. She never gave unsolicited advice, and the suggestions she did give when I asked were always helpful.

As usual, in this situation, I got stellar advice from her on delivering bad news, which I'd like to share with you in this article. All leaders have to share bad news at some point, and doing it unprepared will only make things worse.

So, here's the advice I got and used successfully.

## A Proven Formula: Brace – Hit – Hold

The advice I received gave me a simple formula to follow, which I used in that difficult moment: brace, hit, hold.

**Brace:** Communicate what's happening immediately. So, when the time came, I said, "Guys, I have some bad news."

**Hit:** Go straight to the point. I continued by saying, "I had a talk with Ralf, and there's a possibility that our product won't move forward after this quarter. It all comes down to costs. I'm ready to answer any questions you might have."

**Hold:** Pause and give space to your team to ask questions. So, I just stopped and listened attentively.

When we deliver bad news, we have a natural urge to make things right. I know I had this urge. However, it's important that when you deliver bad news, you don't try to put a positive spin on it. If you sweeten bad news, people will distrust you. I saw this first-hand in a subsequent discussion when a manager tried to spin the news of our product potentially being sunsetted, telling the team to 'look on the bright side'. After this chat, one of my teammates confided in me that he didn't appreciate this approach, he felt that our manager tried to invalidate his feelings.

So, let your team members process the news. Don't try to motivate them to keep working; it will look like you're not on the same team. People have feelings, and all feelings are valid. Leave space for them to process their emotions without trying to sugar-coat the situation. As your team members start to open up, just keep saying "Yeah, I get it." Let them work through it.

After I shared this upsetting news, my team was sad, confused, angry, and frustrated. All valid feelings. I wanted to make these feelings go away for them. But in a situation like this, it's also important to remember that your job is not to hold your teammates' anxiety; they will manage their own. So, I just gave them space to express their disappointments.

## How to Prepare for Giving Bad News

I'm the type of person who loves to prepare for things. It just makes me more relaxed to know I've done all that I could. Before delivering bad news, it's actually advised to write down what you want to say and read it on the day, instead of improvising. It's essential that you are clear, succinct, and straight to the point. You might get nervous when you have to share the unfavourable decision with your team.

If you're in a hybrid or remote setup, delivering the news in an online meeting makes it easier to use your notes. But it's also perfectly fine to have your notes with you in an in-person meeting. You can just say that you're using them as an aid to communicate the news clearly.

Keep it quick and concise, then open up the conversation.

## What to Do After the Bad News

When someone receives bad news, they go through the 5 stages of grief:

- Denial
- Anger
- Bargaining
- Depression
- Acceptance

You can help your team get through this process by creating an opportunity for them to work through it together. After telling my team that our product might get discontinued at the end of the quarter, I waited a day to leave everyone space to process the information. The next day I offered to organise a workshop where we could figure out a way forward, as a team. It's important to process the bad news together and turn it into something productive as soon as possible. Otherwise, there's a risk that people will start to lose their motivation completely.

## Moving Forward

Since we were expected to deliver our quarterly commitments, it was essential to figure out how we move forward, keep our side of the contract and support each other in this period of uncertainty. If you can, it's best for the workshop to take place in person, so you are physically together.

Hold the workshop with the aim of moving forward with this new information. During the workshop, you can identify what you need to do as a team to process the news. Then, you can figure out how to keep delivering on your commitments. It's vital to foster a strong sense of teamwork and the belief that you will overcome this obstacle together. It's natural for motivation to dip after such news. What's most important is that the team continues functioning, no one resigns, and their motivation eventually picks up again.

Once you've crafted a strategy in the workshop, keep checking in with your team. Ask them, "This is what I see. Do we need to find different strategies to cope?" Don't assume to have identified the 'perfect' solution right away.

I'm happy to share that nobody resigned from my team after this troubling news. We came up with a strategy that worked for us, held regular retrospectives to see where we could improve on our processes and in doing so, we implemented next steps that helped us work more effectively and in a better mood. We managed to deliver on our quarterly commitments, but then the product was put on hold, so most of our work over that month and a half never reached our users.

I hope this guide comes in handy the next time you need to deliver bad news. If you're interested, I'd be happy to share exactly how I structured the workshop to help us figure out how to keep up our team's morale.
