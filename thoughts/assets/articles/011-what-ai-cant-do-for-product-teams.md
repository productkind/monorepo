# What AI Can't Do for Product Teams

*Building faster doesn't replace collaboration*

**Date:** August 20, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

The other day I was scrolling through LinkedIn when I saw yet another bold claim: "No need for PMs, because PRDs are dead."

I stared at the screen, irritated.

"This is not how we build products!" I said out loud, to no one but my sofa.

It wasn't the first time I'd seen something like this. A few days earlier, it was "Design is dead." Last week: "The CTO role is dead."

It's easy to see why these posts spread. They're punchy, they sound radical, and they tap into our collective fear of being left behind. But they also miss something fundamental: tools don't kill roles or ways of working. Bad processes do.

Take PRDs (Product Requirement Documents). They emerged in the era of waterfall development, a time when changes were considered expensive, which led to the belief that teams needed detailed specs. The issue isn't that PRDs exist, it's how they're used. Used as a document to hand off from product to design to development to communicate requirements and why we're building something? Useless. Used as a living record of discovery and decisions that the product team made together? Valuable.

If the process of creating a PRD – or any document for that matter – reflects siloed thinking, it won't matter whether it's ten pages, one page, or generated by AI. It will fail its purpose of aligning people.

So instead of asking whether PRDs are dead, we should ask a different question: What's actually slowing us down in building great products?

## The Real Bottleneck: Alignment, Not Speed of Development

In my experience, very few teams fail because they can't build fast enough. What slows us down isn't delivery, but rather a lack of alignment.

Years ago, I joined a company just after they'd pulled the plug on an expensive ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) rollout. The developers weren't the problem. They delivered what they were told to. The issue was that when one engineer asked five different stakeholders the same question, he got five completely different answers.

When I asked a developer who worked on this project to understand what happened, he told me, "I asked five people what needed to be done and all five told me different things."

"There wasn't a person who would have managed and coordinated the whole project," another person shared with me.

No clarity. No alignment. And no one responsible for facilitating collaboration among the stakeholders and product team.

That's the real issue in product development. Not how quickly we can spin up a prototype, but whether we actually agree on what problem we're solving and what success looks like.

Without that shared understanding, all the speed in the world won't help. You just end up getting to the wrong destination faster.

## What AI Really Changes (and What It Doesn't)

AI tools like Cursor, Lovable, v0 or Replit are brilliant. They've lowered the barrier to building something tangible, especially for non-technical people. You can go from an idea to a working prototype in hours instead of weeks. That's a huge step forward.

But speed of execution is rarely the real constraint in product development. AI doesn't magically align stakeholders. It doesn't tell you which problems are worth solving. And it doesn't give your team a shared sense of purpose.

If you already have a clear understanding of the problem, then yes, AI can help you test solutions faster. But if you don't, AI just accelerates the chaos. You'll generate prototypes more quickly, but they'll still be prototypes of the wrong thing.

AI is an amazing accelerator, but it's not a substitute for product thinking.

## So What Should We Do?

If building speed isn't the issue, then our real work is to make sure we're building a valuable solution, and that requires more than documents or tools.

For me, it comes down to three practices I've seen make the biggest difference in teams:

### 1. Make decisions visible.

In too many projects, important choices are made in passing: a quick chat in Slack, a comment in Figma, a decision in a stakeholder meeting not everyone attended. Weeks later, half the team doesn't know why something is being built. One way to tackle this is to instantly communicate decisions with your team (could be in your daily standup), and also to write decisions down. Not as heavy specs, but as a lightweight, living log of what was agreed and why. A single page that everyone can return to. This builds alignment without bureaucracy.

### 2. Invite the team into discovery.

I've always found that when developers join a user interview, they gain empathy you can't capture in a Jira issue. The same goes for designers sitting in on business conversations. When the team shares the discovery work, you don't need to work so hard on alignment later, because people were there when the insights emerged.

### 3. Take time to understand the problem.

There's a natural temptation, especially with AI tools at our fingertips, to rush into solutions. "We'll just prototype and see." But I've learned the cost of jumping into the solution space too quickly: endless rework, frustrated users, wasted energy. Ensuring we understand the problem we want to solve will result in better solutions. A single workshop with your product team to align on the problem, goals, and trade-offs often saves months of building the wrong thing.

These aren't glamorous practices. They don't make for viral LinkedIn posts. But they are what actually keep teams focused and building together.

Not writing long specs. Not throwing documents over the wall. But creating the conditions for a team to work with focus, purpose, and shared understanding.

## Closing Thought

So, are PRDs dead? Is product management dead? Not at all.

What should die are the bad habits: the siloed handoffs, the lack of alignment, the rush to ship without understanding.

AI can help us move faster, and that's worth celebrating. But it's up to us to make sure we're running in the right direction. Use the speed AI gives you to go deeper into discovery, to get clearer on outcomes, and to strengthen alignment in your team.

So the next time you see a post claiming "PRDs are dead" or "PMs are obsolete," take a breath. The specific artefacts we create matter less than how we collaborate around them, the structure we bring to problem-solving, and the product mindset we carry into every decision.

That's the work that never goes out of fashion.
