# How to Run a Team Well-being Workshop

*Processing bad news sometimes requires a workshop – here's a practical structure*

**Date:** August 13, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

We were six weeks away from the end of the quarter. That was the deadline to ship our work to production and the moment we'd find out whether our product would be sunsetted, or continue to evolve. I learned about this news a week earlier and shared it with my team. It was a process. I wrote about delivering this bad news in a previous article.

During an online call with my team, I offered to hold a workshop where we could figure out how to increase our team's morale after learning about this demotivating update. If I'm honest, I wasn't completely certain how to structure this workshop, but I knew it was an important step to help my teammates deliver on our quarterly commitments. And it was important for me that they still find joy in their work, even in the middle of this uncertainty. They agreed to the workshop. So, we scheduled a day when we would all be in the office, so we could do it together in person.

"I'm not sure how to structure this workshop," I confessed to my partner.

"You could talk to this guy I used to work with, he's a pro in facilitating workshops," he helpfully replied.

I immediately jumped on this opportunity. I thought, "I'll take any help I can get."

Before my call with the Workshop Guy, I prepared an outline that I thought might be useful for the session. On our call, I presented it to him. He liked it, and had several follow-up questions. What kind of change do you expect as the result of the workshop? Have you already had a session to give space to just complaining about this unfortunate situation?

He also had constructive feedback. One hour is not enough. Ten people are a lot, you'll have to facilitate it strongly. We don't want to create extra work with this for your team, the goal is well-being.

I came out of this call with many ideas, and a solid outline of what I could do.

## Structure of a Team Well-being Workshop

What follows is the structure of the workshop I held for my team. We had a clear goal: to increase team well-being while living with the uncertainty of whether our product would be developed further in the long run.

### 1. Problem Space

We started the workshop with diving into the problem space. As we hadn't had an official opportunity to vent about this situation as a team, it was important to allow space for people to share what bothered them.

So, I gave 5 minutes for this to happen, and presented them the instructions:

> In pairs, share with each other what bothers you in this situation, what you find hard since the news broke. There are no rules for this section, feel free to complain, rage, vent. Each person has 2 minutes.

Then I asked each pair to leave the room and find a private space. It was important to preserve the meeting room as a solution-focused space, so the complaining had to happen outside. This helped to keep the next part of the session constructive.

### 2. Solution Space

After we had the opportunity to release our frustrations, we moved to something more productive.

We regrouped in the meeting room and started to focus on potential solutions. I gave the following instructions to my teammates:

> Everyone reflects for 3 minutes. Think about what you, or the team, could do that makes you feel better and increases team motivation in the next 1.5 months. Imagine that you're watching a movie of the activities we are doing that help us to feel better. What are these activities?

This enabled us to focus on solutions, instead of things that need to be solved. We left the uncertainty we couldn't control behind and focused on things we actually had the power to do.

After this brief reflection, we moved on to sharing our ideas with the following guidance:

> Everyone shares their best solution and puts it on the whiteboard. (If you have several solution suggestions, and we have time, then you can share after everyone had their turn.)

This allowed everyone to contribute. We imagined what needed to happen in the next month and a half for us to feel good while delivering on our commitments that was non-negotiable.

Once we had each idea on the whiteboard, we started to have a solution-focused discussion that concentrated on what we could actually do that is within our control to improve this situation.

Even though, I got the recommendation from the Workshop Guy to allow each of my teammate to select and own one thing that they felt improved their or the team's well-being, I believed we needed to focus on communal solutions. I thought if we focused on fewer items that required a team effort, the individuals within the team would end up with more value. So, instead of everyone walking away with a personal action item, we together selected four next steps we together wanted to do.

Once we picked these four action items, we quickly agreed on owners and identified what needed to be done to achieve them. For instance, we needed to discuss the details of some of them, so we set specific dates and assigned who was responsible. This is a very important part of any workshop. It's essential that the next steps are clear, we have a shared understanding of what each item means, who needs to do what, and who is responsible for each.

That's why I like to send a summary in bullet-points (in this case via Slack) after the workshop to make sure everyone is on the same page and we have the conclusions recorded.

### 3. Closing Round

Before concluding the workshop, I asked everyone to name one thing they found inspiring today, so people could leave the workshop with a positive mindset. We needed to keep solution-focused and zoom in on what could work, and help in our situation.

## What If People are Not Solution-focused?

It could definitely happen that your team has a hard time moving into thinking about potential solutions. In this scenario, it's still important to acknowledge their hardships, but it's also crucial to turn their minds towards solutions.

You could say, "I hear you. I know it's hard. Now, let's try to focus on potential solutions that are within our control. We can talk more about the issues in our retrospective."

## Next Steps After the Workshop

When you come up with solutions, you rarely have all the data to know they will work out well. That's why it can be helpful to look at these solutions as experiments. So, we agreed to monitor how we feel during the upcoming weeks about these action items we came up with, and share what we were learning from them.

We also agreed that we could change our solutions any time, according to what we learned during the process. It's good to leave flexibility and not to hold onto things that don't work.

## The Learnings

After the quarter ended, my team concluded that the improvement ideas we implemented in our daily work were successful. They helped us to be more focused, decreased the scope of our work and we managed to deliver all of our prioritised outcomes, and deploy them to production.

Even though work on our product got paused at the end of the quarter, we made lemonade out of the lemons we received from management.
