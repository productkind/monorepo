# How I Got Into Product Management – And You Can Too

*How I transitioned to a product role, and the lessons I learned, so you can do the same*

**Date:** June 11, 2025
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

It was a warm February day, I was sitting in a tiny meeting room with my line manager and product owner (PO). I had no idea what they wanted to discuss. It was an unexpected meeting.

"Managing three teams and three different domains is a lot," started my PO. "So, I decided to step back from your scrum team, since that's my newest."

I felt a rush of sadness. I liked the guy.

"Last week, when the new scrum master joined our team and we went around to introduce ourselves," he continued, "I realised that you have a rich experience. You've also been taking a lot off my shoulders by managing the performance measurements and coordinating it with our German boss."

I hadn't even realised that this was something extra I've been doing, and it was above my pay grade. Well, it was nice that he noticed.

"So, I recommended you to our line manager to become the new PO of the team," he concluded.

WHAT?! I was taken aback.

"Wow, I'm flattered," I acted as cool as I could. My insides were swirling. "Does he know that I don't know anything about this?" I thought.

"Is it something that would interest you?" he asked.

"Yes, definitely!" I might have responded a bit too eagerly. After all, it was my goal to get into product. That's why I learned to code in the first place.

"It's a big responsibility and you've only been here for a year," my line manager chimed in.

I got his concern, since I was a junior software engineer in my current position. Even though I had previous experience in account management, marketing, and sales. Nevertheless, I could empathise with his caution. "What would others think if they promoted me to PO? Did anyone else want this job?" I wondered to myself. From the outside, I was just nodding.

"I'm a fast learner," I blurted out.

By the end of the meeting, we agreed that I'll take over the team as a PO, but would need to keep this private until they figured out the right timing. I felt incredibly lucky to be considered for this position that happened to align with my interests and professional goals. We also agreed with my line manager to have another meeting to discuss further details.

I knew that was the time to negotiate my salary increase that comes with a promotion. In the following days, I did some research and thought about what a reasonable ask would be. When the meeting arrived, I was ready to counter his suggestion of an increased salary with a number I thought was fair. Surprisingly, in our discussion, I had to bring the topic of a pay raise up. He didn't come with an offer; he came with excuses. The performance cycles happened in the autumn and the salary adjustments were bound to those. It was really complicated to augment one's salary outside of this cycle. It needed approval from a higher-up. He also insinuated that I wouldn't succeed, he didn't trust my skills and my lack of product experience, and it was a big gamble for him. All that he had to back those opinions up? I was a young, soft-spoken woman. Of course, he didn't say this last part, but it was a boys' club with blatant sexism, explicit comments in the kitchen about female colleagues' outfits.

I left that meeting deflated, but with fire in me to prove him wrong. I knew I could do a good job. My desire to get into product was the reason I did a developer bootcamp and ended up at that company a year ago as a software engineer. I wanted to understand how software was made. I had a business and management background, but I didn't have a technical one prior to the bootcamp.

Soon enough, my "promotion" was announced. Sure, there were a couple of surprised people, but I was ready to prove myself. I was incredibly grateful for the opportunity to become a product owner.

I got some scrum and product owner book recommendations from my line manager and joined a circle of product owners and scrum masters who met monthly to share experiences and challenges. The sessions were moderated by an Agile coach.

I thought if I kept my head down, worked hard, then he'd see me and finally offer me the salary the position required. To make sure this happened, I advocated for myself and reminded him in subsequent one-on-ones that I've been performing well. I wish I could tell you that he eventually believed in me and appreciated my work, but it never happened. Even after I got stellar feedback from several people and he even acknowledged that I was doing a good job, he didn't advocate for increasing my salary.

After 6 months working as a product owner for a junior developer's salary, I got enough of being underpaid. When I resigned, my line manager was surprised; he didn't understand what he could have done differently. This signalled to me that he hadn't thought for a moment that it was exploitative not to compensate me fairly for my work. Was it because I am a woman? Or because I was young? Or because I was nice, so he thought it was easy to take advantage of my people-pleaser tendencies? I have guesses, but I'll never know for sure.

## Why did I start a Substack?

This all happened over 7 years ago, and the thought of his entitled behaviour still riles me up. I never want you, dear Reader, to feel "less than" because your manager is too afraid to advocate for you. This is why I am starting this Substack.

Building a tech product requires a lot of collaboration, a vast range of skills, and an open mind to keep learning and improving. I love this field. Being a product manager is the first role that I really enjoy doing since I started my career. Since this initial experience in product, I worked as a product manager in a startup, mid-sized company, and another enterprise, all in different industries. Now, I am coaching product people and building digital products in my own business, called productkind.

When I started out, I wished there was more to-the-point, actionable advice on how to do each step of the product development process, but it was hard to come by. The internet is filled with broad and general advice. You should do user research! Okay, but how do I get started? What do I ask? You should keep your developers motivated! Ugh, duh! But what actual steps do I do when people get demotivated through the ups and downs of building a product and dealing with the ebbs and flows of a business?

There is also a lot of dogmatic advice – e.g. the rules to scrum, kanban –, but I'd like to show you that questioning the status quo and asking the "why" behind most things are good things. Knowledge happens by exercising critical thinking.

I would like to be your mentor in this process so you can trust your skills and know that you belong.

This space is not only for product managers but developers, designers, and anyone who is interested in building valuable products and desires to know more actionable steps and techniques on how to make it a reality.

So, I would like to invite you to subscribe and join the conversation around what exact techniques we can apply to create more value for your users, business, and society.

## How to get into product?

There are many ways to get into a product role. The easiest approach is joining a company that has your desired role and working towards that. Here are the key steps to do so.

### Share your experience

Even if you think it's not relevant for the specific job, all your experience is relevant! Identify what your transferable skills are. Communication, teamwork, stakeholder management, leadership skills, giving feedback, saying no, facilitation skills, technical understanding, design thinking, business knowledge are just some of the skills you might have picked up in previous roles and are essential for being a product manager.

### Be assertive and take initiative

Even if something might not be your job, if it needs solving, go for it. I am not advocating for being the person who does everything that others don't want to do. You need to identify what the activities are that you'd like to take charge of in your potential new role. For me, it was directly dealing with our German boss to coordinate something central to our product – performance.

### Make your work visible

Mention your work on daily standups, demos, in casual conversations. I know, I hate to toot my own horn, but it's essential. And it can be done in a nice way. Disguise it as an update or as an interesting anecdote. Unfortunately, we cannot wait for others to realise what great job we've been doing.

### Find a person who believes in you

This person for me was my PO. Even though my line manager was the one who needed to make the final decision about my promotion, it all happened because of my PO's trust in me. Let that person know your aspirations. Actually, let everyone know your aspirations. Then they might think of you when a new position opens up. It would have been stellar advice for my past self too, as I didn't share with anyone that I wanted to work in product, because I didn't think it was possible at that company.

### Start preparing for your thought-after role

Even if you haven't got your desired role yet, you can start preparing for it. Even though I wanted to become a product manager, I wasn't learning about the specificities of that role before I got it. I was too immersed in learning software development, the practices of performance measurement, testing, and all related topics. So, it might be challenging to study different fields, but it can come in handy if you can show that you are prepared when the opportunity comes. Unfortunately, I couldn't demonstrate this – if I could, then maybe my line manager would have trusted me more.

### Say yes to the role, even without the money

Accept the new role that helps you achieve your dreams, even if they don't compensate you for it. Once you have this experience on your CV, it's way easier to find a place that better aligns with your values. At the end of the day, I was grateful to get the PO position and I wouldn't have refused it, even if my line manager was straightforward about not giving a salary increase. It's also important though to recognise when you are being exploited and not to stay stuck in disadvantageous situations.
