# What We Learned from Building Digital Products: Lessons and Pivots

*Looking back at last year, I'm sharing what worked and what most certainly didn't, so you can do even better.*

**Date:** July 14, 2026
**Author:** Kinga Magyar

---

Hi, it's Kinga. I've spent the last eight years creating digital products as a product manager. Now, I'm developing Little Parrot, a platform designed to help women build their own products through practical micro-courses.

It's the first digital product of my two-person company, productkind. Our mission is to close the gender gap in AI adoption.

We are in the middle of 2026, and this made me reflect.

2025 was the first full year of my micro-business era where I experimented with various activities. One of them was building a digital product, Little Parrot, that offers micro-courses to women to help them build digital products.

It didn't start out that way.

First, we focused on product managers, because I saw a gap in courses that develop practical skills.

But I yearned to do something more mission driven. And since I now have the freedom to work on what I want, we pivoted to helping women build digital products with AI.

I know AI is currently a massive buzzword. However, if it's used well, it truly can speed things up and empower people to build digital products even if they cannot code yet.

## What Didn't Work

### 1. Posting Comics on Instagram

We consistently posted three product-development-related comics and a carousel of my latest articles on Instagram each week for 19 weeks. We started from 0 followers and in January this year we had 33. We posted 76 posts and we're following 15 accounts.

[image: How our prolific Instagram account looked at the beginning of this year]

I don't need to look at any industry benchmarks to know that is a tiny number.

Yes, we haven't promoted this account much. No paid ads whatsoever and only some promotion on Reddit, but not in the channels that our real target audience frequents.

Many Reddit groups have very strict rules on self-promotion.

Our goal with the Instagram account was to share these articles on there as well, provide value through comics and articles, and ultimately guide users over to Little Parrot.

Instead, we learned that carousels don't get pushed out to non-followers, so they're actually a bad way to get discovered on Instagram.

### 2. Posting Comics on Substack Notes

To get more out of the comics we created, since they took quite significant time investment, I also posted them on Substack Notes.

There wasn't much engagement.

Since then, I've read that images and videos are not shown to as many people on Notes as written content.

[image: One of our comics about building a minimum viable product (MVP). You can also see our "old" productkind branding here. We iterated a lot on that as well.]

### 3. Freestyling My Work Days

I'm the kind of person who loves to organise things, especially digitally. Organising user interview findings or creating structure for a workshop is my happy place, especially if a cup of light-roasted Ethiopian coffee is involved.

At the same time, living within too much structure kills my creativity. I need space to thrive and being confined to a rigid work schedule suffocates me.

So when I went solo, I decided to take each day as it came. Whatever I felt like that day I'd work on. Quickly enough, this proved to be exhausting. Every day having to make a decision on what to do out of the unlimited options felt draining.

So this year, we're trying something a bit more structured. We're trialling planning ahead for a single week. It also helps that I'm no longer working alone, because we are two of us now, so I can share and discuss ideas. I'll write about our findings of this new workflow in a future post.

## What Worked

Okay, now let's look at the activities that we consider a success.

### 1. Writing Articles on Substack

I convinced myself several times that it was a bad idea to start my newsletter.

> "There are already too many people doing the same."

> "Who do you think you are?"

> "You're late, you should have started earlier."

Do these thoughts sound familiar?

If they do, then I encourage you to tell those limiting thoughts to shut up and just do the thing you want to do.

Everyone has that project they think about putting out into the world, but then their limiting thoughts intervene. I have them even after starting this newsletter and getting encouraging feedback.

I got feedback that the articles have been helpful for people. My goal with writing was exactly that: to help people feel more confident in building digital products.

### 2. Collaborating with Lovable

I started using Lovable in January 2025. It allowed me to create products without having to code that felt very powerful. In the spring, they put out a call for Lovable Shipped, a six-week hackathon. That's when I became a subscriber and started working with it every week.

This was the time I started building our microlearning platform, Little Parrot.

I ended up finishing at #40 in the competition. This placement hasn't contributed to our business in any way, but the accountability the program provided helped me to make steady progress on this product for six consecutive weeks.

In the autumn, Lovable organised another competition, specifically for women. SheBuilds on Lovable was a 48-hour buildathon where I experimented with a new product.

It was intense, but the community felt different than in the Shipped competition. It didn't feel like a competition at all, but rather like a support group.

Afterwards, I had a chat with Whitney Menarcheck, Lovable's amazing community manager. She's so passionate about encouraging women to build products to make their side-businesses come true or enhance their existing venture.

This competition inspired me to change the direction of Little Parrot, and to focus on women who can't code yet.

### 3. Giving Talks and Running Workshops at Conferences

When I applied to several conferences at the beginning of the year, I didn't have Little Parrot yet. I wasn't building productkind with the mission we currently have to close the gender gap in AI adoption.

All I wanted to do was practise public speaking. I gave several talks inside various companies I worked at. But speaking at a conference is different. Up to that point, I had only spoken at one conference.

I got accepted to five conferences in 2025. I gave talks at two of them and ran workshops at three.

It turned out that connecting with your target audience at conferences is a good way to better understand their pain points and also to acquire new users.

But for this to work, you need to be crystal clear on who your target audience is. We went to two conferences where the main audience was backend developers. It was interesting, we learned things, but we had very low conversion rate on both our newsletter (this one) and our courses on Little Parrot.

Meanwhile, when I gave a talk at a product management conference, a significant number of people subscribed to this newsletter (thank you if you're reading this!). I'll share more in a future article about this experience and useful techniques you might want to adapt for your business.

### 4. Building a Digital Product with Lovable

I started building Little Parrot with Lovable. The "0 to 1" part felt really powerful: it was quick, there was something to test with potential users right away.

Then, as the product and its complexity grew, came the more frustrating bit. Lovable has improved a lot in this past year and they are constantly making the product better. But when I was integrating Stripe as the payment provider last summer, things got really frustrating.

I had to enlist the help of my software developer partner – who since joined my venture. Now, I know, not everyone has a software developer on speed-dial. That's partially the reason we're building approachable, practical courses on vibe coding (shameless plug: check out our courses on Little Parrot).

Overall, I learned that there are several techniques that prevent you from getting stuck in a loop of fixing things that continuously break. We also built these learnings into our courses. Using a vibe coding tool to build your digital product speeds you up, so you can test its value quicker.
