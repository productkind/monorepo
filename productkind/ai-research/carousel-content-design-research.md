## The one caveat that governs everything below

Almost all of this evidence was measured on narrated instructional videos
and data-visualisation slideshows, not on silent, swipe-paced social
carousels. The findings are therefore well-founded principles rather than
carousel-specific proof, and the effect sizes are generally small-to-
moderate. Nothing in the research validates a sub-minute length as optimal:
effective microlearning studies clustered at one to fifteen minutes, and
critics specifically question whether genuinely complex topics survive that
much condensation. This is the honest reason our carousels should teach one
real idea and send people to the full course or article for the rest, not
try to teach the whole thing.

## Why the format works (instructional design and learning science)

Chunking content into single-idea units works because it keeps information
within the limits of working memory, which is the mechanism Cognitive Load
Theory describes. This is the reason one-idea-per-slide is not just a style
choice but a learning principle.

Microlearning genuinely teaches, it does not merely entertain. A PRISMA-
guided systematic review of forty studies found consistent positive effects
on knowledge, behaviour, and motivation, and a randomised experiment with
104 MBA students found that people who received an article's content as a
bite-sized module scored significantly higher on a knowledge test than
people who read the identical content as a document (6.6 versus 5.6 out of
10, p < 0.05). The catch is that the RCT was small and single-topic, so we
should read this as encouraging rather than decisive.

Three of Mayer's multimedia-learning principles give a design recipe, all
verified here. The multimedia principle says people learn better from words
combined with graphics than from words alone, which supports a visual
carousel over a text-only summary, but only when the graphic explains or
organises the idea; a decorative image gives no benefit and can actively
distract. The coherence principle says that adding interesting-but-
irrelevant material ("seductive details") significantly harms learning
because it competes for limited attention, which is what licenses
aggressive cutting when we condense. The segmenting principle says complex
material is learned better when broken into short, learner-paced,
progressively revealed parts, which is a fair description of a carousel the
reader swipes through at their own pace.

## How to choose what to extract (educational content design)

Selection should centre on a single, specific, action-oriented objective
per unit: what the reader should be able to do or understand afterwards.
The forty-study review proposes a design framework built on this (its six
principles are a specific objective, bite-sized content, a delivery
timeframe, interactive content, personalisation, and the right medium), and
a separate study proposes a three-stage flow for each micro-unit:
Consumption (present the condensed idea), Conceptualisation (connect it to
what the learner already knows), and Confirmation (test or reflect to
solidify it). Both frameworks are author proposals the papers themselves
flag as not yet independently validated, so we treat them as useful
structure rather than proven law.

Extraction is a decomposable, trainable skill, not opaque intuition. The
foundational summarisation research (Hidi & Anderson) shows that skilled
summarisers differ from unskilled ones along identifiable lines: awareness
of the task, sensitivity to what is important, and the ability to condense
and to write topic sentences. The single most useful distinction for us is
between a "writer-based" summary (the author's own notes, compressed) and a
"reader-based" summary (written and restructured for an audience). A
carousel must be reader-based: mirroring the article's own section order
onto slides is the writer-based mistake, because the article's structure
was built for someone who has committed to the whole piece, not for a
swiping reader.

Pairing an abstract idea with a concrete example measurably improves
learning. In a randomised study of 170 undergraduates, people who studied
definitions together with concrete examples were better at recognising
which concept a new scenario illustrated than people who studied
definitions alone, and the benefit transferred to previously unseen
examples (though that transfer effect was small and marginal, d = 0.30,
p = 0.0489). The practical reading is that every abstract point on a slide
should be shown in the concrete, not just stated.

## How to sequence the slides (visual storytelling and narrative design)

Slide sequencing follows an evidence-based grammar, verified from Hullman
et al.'s controlled experiments on narrative visualisation. Each transition
should change only one thing: in a study of 143 participants, viewers
strongly preferred transitions where a single dimension changed over
transitions where two or three changed at once, and professional designers
were found to hold everything constant except the one new element from
slide to slide. Consistency lets the reader tie the slides into a coherent
story instead of being overloaded. Repeating a transition pattern across
the sequence (parallelism) significantly improved how well people
remembered the sequence (p = 0.002), which is why giving the idea slides a
recognisable, repeating shape is worth doing. The main limit on this
finding is that it was tested on data-visualisation slideshows with one
dataset, so the specific ordering preferences may not transfer literally to
concept- or story-based carousels, even though the one-change and
parallelism principles are more general.

## The practical method this adds up to

1. Define the one takeaway before drafting: what the reader should be able
   to do or understand afterwards. It drives selection and ordering.
2. Extract as a reader-based summary. Restructure for the audience and lead
   with the payoff idea; do not walk the article's own paragraph order.
3. Cut everything that does not serve the takeaway, however interesting.
4. Keep one idea per slide, revealed progressively.
5. Show each abstract point with a concrete example.
6. Make every visual explain part of the idea, never decorate.
7. Move one step per slide, in a repeating rhythm.
8. Add one do-this-or-decide beat (a question, a "which is you?", a small
   step) before the CTA, so the piece teaches rather than only tells.
9. End by opening out into the reader's next step: the full course or
   article.

## Open questions the evidence could not settle

The evidence base does not tell us whether these effects replicate for
silent, passively-scrolled social carousels as opposed to the narrated
video and instructional slideshows they were measured on. It gives no
validated lower bound below which condensation stops teaching and becomes
mere familiarity. It confirms that sensitivity to importance is trainable
but gives no operational rule for choosing the single most valuable idea
when several compete. And it does not establish whether the data-
visualisation sequencing rules transfer to narrative or comic carousels.
