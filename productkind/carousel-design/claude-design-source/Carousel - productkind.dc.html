I love product management. It's such an exciting mix of business, technology, design and psychology.

But I was tired of the corporate politics, and of building products executives wanted while the people using them had different ideas.

Call me idealistic, but I wanted a positive impact beyond shareholder revenue.

{{ handle }}03 / 08

I started with an executive coaching programme. Did I enjoy coaching people? Yes, the nice ones. 😅 I also knew I wanted the freedom to work from anywhere.

{{ handle }}04 / 08

Then I discovered vibe coding: building software with AI tools, without having to know how to code. I started building products, even though I don't know TypeScript. It was such an empowering feeling that I gave talks and ran workshops about it, and started building Little Parrot, a microlearning app.

{{ handle }}05 / 08

In the autumn I joined SheBuilds on Lovable, a 48-hour hackathon for women. There I saw a sea of professional women with ideas they wanted to build.

But most vibe coding content is made by tech bros, for tech bros.

{{ handle }}06 / 08

This has happened before. The term 'software engineer' was coined by a woman, Margaret Hamilton, in 1963. By 1984, women earned 37% of computer science degrees in the US. Then personal computers were marketed almost exclusively to boys and men, and the numbers began to drop.

{{ handle }}07 / 08
14%

Now a new technology is here: generative AI. Yet only 14% of Lovable's users are women, and you can only benefit from AI by using it. So we've refocused our two-person company, productkind, on one mission: closing the gender gap in AI adoption.

{{ handle }}08 / 08

Nobody has ten years of experience in vibe coding yet. We're all starting from the same line. I'll be sharing what I try, what works, and what definitely doesn't in my newsletter, Thoughts by productkind.
