# Substack AI detection / losing our uniqueness

**Angle:** Substack's new AI-detection feature (built on Pangram) as a way in to a worry Kinga notices in herself: AI-generated writing is homogenising everyone's voice, even pulling non-native speakers towards borrowed American idioms.
**Key point:** Keep a list of the phrases you never used to say and read your drafts back to cut them, so your writing doesn't blur into everyone else's. Posts alongside the Pangram chart.

---

Substack just introduced a new feature to identify AI-generated writing, using Pangram.

I welcome this change.

Coincidentally, I read this week that AI-generated content is everywhere on social media. Probably you won't be surprised, but LinkedIn came out worst, with more than 40% of longform posts flagged as fully AI-generated.

We're losing our uniqueness, every post gets more homogenous. Certain phrases used to belong to a subset of people. In podcasts I kept hearing American native speakers say "here's the thing". Since GenAI, non-native speakers use American idioms in writing they've never used before.

Sometimes, when I generate text with AI, I no longer remember how I would write something. I just know this isn't it.

And when I write, I catch myself thinking about phrases like "why it matters" that I've almost never used before AI writing flooded our feeds.

What do you think, would it serve LinkedIn if we could scan every post and comment for AI use?
